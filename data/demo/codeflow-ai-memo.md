# Investment Memo — CodeFlow AI

**Date:** September 2025
**Sector:** Developer Tools
**Stage:** Seed ($3M round)
**Author:** Investment team
**Recommendation: PASS**

## Company overview
CodeFlow AI builds an AI pair-programmer that lives in the IDE and the terminal. It autocompletes code, explains unfamiliar repositories, and can execute multi-file refactors from a natural-language instruction. Think "Copilot, but agentic across the whole repo."

## Founders
- **Daniel Osei** — CEO. Former senior engineer at a mid-size fintech; built internal dev-tooling.
- **Lena Hoffmann** — CTO. Ex-ML engineer; published work on code representation learning.

Strong technical founders. Genuine domain insight into developer pain. Likeable, coachable.

## Problem & solution
Developers lose hours context-switching and reading unfamiliar code. CodeFlow indexes the whole repo and offers repo-aware completions and an agent that can carry out refactors. The demo is genuinely impressive — it correctly refactored a gnarly auth module live.

## Traction
- 6,000 individual developers signed up via a free VS Code extension.
- ~900 weekly active users.
- $11k MRR from a $15/mo Pro tier — but **logo churn is ~12% monthly** on the paid tier.
- No team/enterprise contracts yet.

## Market
Crowded and fast-moving: GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Codeium, Cody, and several well-funded startups. The category is real and large, but incumbents have distribution (GitHub) and frontier-lab partnerships.

## Strengths
- Excellent founding team with real technical depth.
- Product demo quality is high; the agentic refactor is differentiated today.
- Bottoms-up developer signups show genuine pull.

## Risks & concerns
- **Weak team/seat adoption** — usage is individual hobbyists, not teams. The paid retention (12% monthly churn) suggests the product is a "nice-to-have," not yet a daily driver.
- **Distribution disadvantage** — competing head-on with GitHub/Microsoft and Cursor, who own the surface area and move fast.
- **Thin moat** — the differentiation (repo-aware agent) is being rapidly copied by better-funded competitors.
- No clear wedge into teams, where the budget actually sits.

## Decision
**PASS.** The team is excellent and we'd want to back them on a future company. But at seed we need to see either defensible distribution or evidence the product becomes a daily, team-level habit. Today the adoption is shallow (individual, high-churn) in the single most competitive category in dev tools. Revisit if they crack team adoption or find a defensible wedge.
